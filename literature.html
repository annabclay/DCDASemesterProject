Literature Review

Understanding the Academic Context: Reality TV, Authenticity, and Audience Engagement

1. Reality Television as Constructed Narrative

The foundation of this research rests on scholarship that challenges the notion of reality television as unmediated documentation. Mittell (2015) in Complex TV: The Poetics of Contemporary Television Storytelling argues that modern television—including reality formats—employs sophisticated narrative techniques that blur the line between documentary and fiction. Rather than capturing reality, producers actively construct narrative arcs through editing decisions.

Hill (2015) in Reality TV: Audiences and Popular Factual Television provides comprehensive analysis of how reality TV production creates meaning. She identifies three critical stages where "reality" is constructed: pre-production (casting and scenario design), production (filming decisions and participant direction), and post-production (editing and music selection). Each stage involves editorial choices that shape how audiences interpret events.

Key Insight: Reality television is not a window onto unfiltered life—it is a carefully constructed narrative product. The appearance of authenticity is itself a production achievement.

2. Authenticity, Performance, and the "Reality" Paradox

Skeggs and Wood (2012) in Reacting to Reality Television: Performance, Audience and Value explore how reality TV contestants navigate impossible expectations: to be both authentically themselves and perform in ways that are television-worthy. This creates what they call the "authenticity performance"—a state where the most authentic moment is often the one that has been carefully crafted.

Related to this is the work on parasocial relationships in digital culture. Horton and Wohl (1956) first theorized parasocial interactions—one-sided relationships where audiences feel connected to media personalities. Contemporary research by Dibble, Hartmann, and Rosaen (2016) shows that reality TV intensifies parasocial bonding by creating illusions of intimacy and genuine access. Audiences feel they "know" contestants because they've witnessed their unfiltered emotions, even when those emotions have been strategically amplified by production.

Key Insight: Vulnerability creates parasocial bonds. When audiences believe they're witnessing authentic emotion, they develop stronger attachments to contestants and increased investment in the narrative.

3. Editing Techniques and Emotional Manipulation

The technical aspects of how emotion is constructed through editing are explored in film and television theory. Walter Murch's In the Blink of an Eye (2001) demonstrates how editing creates meaning through rhythm and pacing. Fast cuts create urgency and anxiety; slow cuts allow emotional contemplation. Music layering intensifies emotional response through a mechanism called "emotional congruence"—when sound and image align, viewers experience heightened emotional resonance.

Hessler and Smith (2015) specifically apply these principles to television in their article "Emotion in Motion: How Television Creates Feeling." They document how reality TV producers use:

  • Reaction shots: Inserting close-ups of others' responses to amplify the emotional significance of a moment
  • Strategic silence: Removing music momentarily to create tension, then reintroducing it to heighten climax
  • Cut pacing variation: Rapid cuts during conflict, slower pacing during vulnerability to encourage viewer empathy
  • Music selection: Specific instrumentation primes emotional categories (strings for sadness, percussion for conflict)
Key Insight: Emotion is not simply captured by cameras—it is synthesized through post-production. Technical editing choices have measurable effects on emotional response.

4. Social Media Engagement and Vulnerability Economics

Contemporary research on platform algorithms and emotional content reveals that vulnerability generates engagement. Zulli and Zulli (2020) in "Extending the Internet meme: Conceptualizing cybernetic creativity and the evolution of the meme" demonstrate that emotionally intense content spreads faster on social media. Vulnerability—when framed as authenticity—is algorithmically promoted because it generates shares, comments, and discussion.

Driessen (2017) examines the "attention economy" in her article "Affective Labor in Entertainment: The Case of Reality Television," arguing that reality TV contestants are performing emotional labor for audiences. Their vulnerability becomes a commodity. Networks monetize vulnerable moments through advertising and algorithmic promotion on social platforms. The more emotionally intense a scene, the higher its advertising value and algorithmic visibility.

This creates a direct causal link: vulnerability → audience engagement → social media virality → advertising revenue. Understanding this economic incentive structure is crucial to explaining why producers strategically amplify emotional moments.

Key Insight: In the attention economy, vulnerability is monetizable. Platforms and networks profit from emotional content, creating financial incentives to amplify and strategically deploy vulnerability.

5. Narrative Structure and Emotional Arcs

Story structure theory, as outlined in Vogler's The Hero's Journey (2007) and adapted for television by Mittell, demonstrates that narratives require emotional peaks and valleys to maintain engagement. Reality TV producers apply these principles consciously. They identify vulnerable contestants early and structure their screen time to create a narrative arc where vulnerability increases as the season progresses, peaking just before finales.

This is particularly evident in franchise television where multiple seasons allow producers to refine these techniques. Contestants who deliver the most compelling emotional arcs—those who are most vulnerable at strategically important moments—receive more screen time and become "fan favorites." This creates a feedback loop where vulnerability becomes a career strategy for contestants seeking screen time and post-show opportunities.

Porter (2017) in "The Editing of Television" explains how producers use narrative structure to guide emotional response. By placing vulnerable moments at specific points in episodes (cliffhangers before commercial breaks, climaxes in episode 8 of 10), they maximize audience investment and retention.

Key Insight: Vulnerability is structurally necessary for narrative engagement. Producers deliberately position emotional moments to maximize their impact on story arcs.

6. Ethical Concerns and Contestant Consent

As this research demonstrates that vulnerability is strategically amplified, ethical questions arise. Calvert and Murray (2022) in "The Ethics of Reality Television: Consent, Exploitation, and Mental Health" argue that contestants often do not fully understand how their vulnerability will be edited, amplified, and presented to audiences. They consent to appear on camera, but not necessarily to the specific narrative or emotional portrayal that results.

This becomes particularly acute with younger contestants. Javidi and Strong (2021) examine the case of child performers on shows like Dance Moms, arguing that children cannot meaningfully consent to the amplification and monetization of their emotional vulnerability. The production becomes a form of affective labor exploitation.

Additionally, research on post-show mental health outcomes suggests that contestants who have had their vulnerability heavily amplified and publicly scrutinized often experience trauma. The "reality" of reality TV—the real psychological costs—are often not discussed in promotional materials.

Key Insight: Strategic amplification of vulnerability raises consent and exploitation issues, particularly with younger contestants. The production benefits from vulnerability; the individual often bears the psychological costs.

7. Gaps in Existing Literature

While existing scholarship establishes that reality TV is constructed and that vulnerability generates engagement, there remains a gap in directly measuring the relationship between specific editing techniques and audience perception of authenticity. Most studies focus on either production practices OR audience response, but fewer explicitly connect editing choices to engagement metrics and viewer perception of authenticity.

Additionally, while ethical concerns have been raised, there is limited quantitative research on how audiences themselves evaluate whether vulnerability is authentic or constructed. Do viewers actually recognize editing's role in emotional amplification, or does the illusion of authenticity persist even when they're aware of production practices?

Finally, existing research tends to analyze established franchises (The Bachelor, Survivor). There is less scholarship on how these principles apply across different reality TV genres or how production techniques evolve as audiences become more media literate.

Contribution of This Research: This project bridges these gaps by directly connecting editing techniques to audience perception and engagement metrics, using both qualitative analysis (case studies) and quantitative data (survey results) to demonstrate how production choices shape the experience and monetization of vulnerability.

Conclusion: What We Know and What This Research Adds

What existing literature establishes:

  • Reality TV is constructed through deliberate production choices
  • Vulnerability creates parasocial relationships and audience investment
  • Editing techniques directly influence emotional response
  • Emotional content drives social media engagement and monetization
  • Ethical concerns exist around contestant consent and exploitation

What remains unclear (and what this project addresses):

  • How do audiences evaluate the authenticity of edited moments when aware of production practices?
  • What is the quantitative relationship between specific editing techniques and perception of authenticity?
  • Do audiences recognize that their engagement is being strategically engineered?
  • How do these dynamics vary across different reality TV genres?

This research project uses qualitative case study analysis combined with audience survey data to directly address these questions, providing empirical evidence of how strategic editing shapes both vulnerability and monetization in reality television.
